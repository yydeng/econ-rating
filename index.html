Working paper

Ranking Scholars When Journals and Citations Matter

Yangyi Deng Department of Decisions, Operations and Technology, The Chinese University of Hong Kong dengyangyi@link.cuhk.edu.hk https://yydeng.github.io/
Chiu Yu Ko Department of Decisions, Operations and Technology and Department of Economics, The Chinese University of Hong Kong chiuyuko@cuhk.edu.hk https://kochiuyu.github.io/

Abstract

Evaluating scholarly output is central to academic resource allocation, yet the profession remains divided between metrics based on journal prestige and those based on citation impact. We develop a unified axiomatic framework that integrates ordinal journal tiers with cardinal citation counts. Within it, we characterize two complementary families of scholar-ranking rules: the journal-based Bean-Counting family, which applies explicit citation thresholds to journal placements, and the citation-based Asymmetric Constant Elasticity of Substitution (CES) aggregators, which reward publication breadth within recognized journals and citation depth outside them. Our application to rank economists at QS Top 100 universities (2023–2026) yields three main findings: journal-based and citation-based rules diverge sharply; this divergence varies substantially across fields; and rules anchored to top-tier journal placement are more robust to field-specific citation rescaling.

Research Motivation

Why measure scholarly impact in two complementary ways?

Journal placement is an ex-ante signal of quality. Citations are an ex-post signal of impact. Each omits what the other captures, and fields weigh them differently. We combine both in one framework with two complementary ranking families, plus an interactive tool to see how rankings change.

At a glance

Two complementary rule families

The paper formalizes two families of scholar-ranking rules:

  • Bean-Counting (BC) — count publications in recognized journals, with citation thresholds adjusting credit for placements outside top tiers.
  • CES (CES) — reward publication breadth within recognized journals and citation depth outside them via an elasticity-of-substitution aggregator.
